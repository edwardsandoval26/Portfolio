## Problem Statement

Parkinson's disease (PD) is the leading cause of disability worldwide, yet diagnosis often occurs late, only after significant neuronal loss has already taken place[cite: 416, 428, 434]. While oculomotor fixation abnormalities (such as micro-tremors and atypical frequencies) are sensitive biomarkers for early detection, traditional diagnostic methods are often invasive, subjective, or rely on complex equipment[cite: 418, 419, 435, 443].

Current deep learning approaches for PD detection typically require **large labeled datasets and expert annotations**, which are scarce and can introduce expert bias—leading to diagnostic errors in up to 24% of early-stage cases[cite: 420, 448, 508]. The goal of this work was to develop a **self-supervised Riemannian representation** capable of extracting discriminative fixation patterns without the need for manual labels[cite: 421, 456].

## Dataset

- **Source:** Non-invasive, markerless video recordings of eye fixation exercises[cite: 469, 164].
- **Samples:** 26 subjects (13 patients with confirmed PD, 13 healthy controls)[cite: 426, 161].
- **Class distribution:** Balanced demographic features; patients spanned multiple progression stages (Hoehn-Yahr scales 2.5 to 4)[cite: 162, 171].
- **Modality:** 2D spatio-temporal video slices extracted at four radial directions ($0^\circ, 45^\circ, 90^\circ, 135^\circ$) around the iris center[cite: 458, 520, 168].

A data augmentation strategy using color channel independence and spatial flips expanded the dataset to **16,640 slice samples** for robust training[cite: 174].

## Pipeline

![Pipeline](/images/projects/AESPD/Full_Slicing_pipeline.png)

**Step 1 — Feature Extraction** Eye fixation videos are transformed into 2D spatio-temporal slices[cite: 458, 520]. These slices are processed through a pre-trained CNN (such as VGG19, ConvNext, or Xception) to retrieve deep activation maps[cite: 524, 177, 179].

**Step 2 — SPD Descriptor Construction** Feature activation maps are summarized into **Symmetric Positive Definite (SPD) covariance matrices**[cite: 422, 526]. These descriptors capture compact second-order relationships between features while remaining robust to noise[cite: 459, 483, 527].

**Step 3 — Self-Supervised Representation Learning** A **Riemannian autoencoder** learns to compress and reconstruct the SPD descriptors[cite: 461, 530]. The encoder utilizes **BiMap** and **ReEig** layers to project data into a low-dimensional embedding while preserving the underlying manifold geometry[cite: 423, 424, 532, 566].

**Step 4 — Classification** The learned SPD embeddings are projected to Euclidean space using a **Riemannian logarithm map**, vectorized, and used to train a **Support Vector Machine (SVM)** to distinguish:  
- Parkinsonian patterns  
- Healthy control patterns[cite: 427, 560, 154].

Despite being **self-supervised**, the proposed architecture achieves outstanding discriminative results, outperforming standard diagnostic reliance on subjective motor patterns[cite: 427, 435, 456]. The model effectively captures hidden, variable, and complex fixation patterns, demonstrating that geometry-aware deep learning can provide a powerful digital biomarker for Parkinson’s detection with limited labeled data[cite: 16, 462, 466, 468].
